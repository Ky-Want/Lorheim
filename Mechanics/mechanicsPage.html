Souls and Magic: An overview of how they work and why

A couple quick notes on magic:

First of all, magic in this setting will be run using a point based system rather than spell slots. Basically, players will get a certain number of points per day to spend on spells and spell-like abilities. There isn't a set amount of damage dice, buffs, etc. that will determine the exact outcome every time. Instead there will be a base line and a number of points it costs to use that spell or ability. Players can use more points than needed to increase the spells potency, but should be wary of going overboard since points can't be regained until after a long rest.

Magic users will be very limited due to the Rift Crisis. As such the general population will mainly be made up of those who can use martial magic (alchemists, rune keepers, rogues, juggernauts, etc.). Magic casters will be highly limited and sought after if they are found out.

Similar to Arcane mages, Naturae mages will be sought out if discovered as well, but rather than for the purpose of being recruited into the military they will most likely be either imprisoned or exterminated due to the high risk they pose to what civilizations remain.



Souls
Souls exist in every living thing, they are what separates the living from the dead. As a creature develops and grows, so to does it's soul. Not all souls are the same though, much like certain evolutionary traits some souls grow and develop to the point of being flexible and capable of processing things like mana, like a mages soul, while some remain in a set state and only exist to prove that a creature is alive. Plants are a good example of this. Every living plant has a soul, and while these souls aren't flexible enough to manipulate magic, they do still take in mana. Though, the way they take in mana is a little different for some. For some plants, namely alchemical ones, they have evolved to absorb some of the mana that enters their soul like they would nutrients, resulting in the various types of plants useable in potions.

Souls are kind of like a bunch of wires. They act as a magnet and conduit for mana. Normally, mana would simply enter the soul and leave without any change, but when a soul is developed and flexible enough it can process the mana into something more useful.

Now for the reason souls act like magnets for arcane mana. This one is rather simple, the reason for this is Naturae mana, or the essence of souls. Naturae and arcane mana are fundamentally different, but also just similar enough to not clash with each other like moody siblings whenever they come into contact with one another. When arcane mana encounters naturae mana, it's drawn in, but once it enters the thing giving off that essence, the naturae mana naturally pushes it out rather than mixing, much like oil and water.

Each soul is different, so there's always going to be a special case, but these are the base rules for souls.




Magic
Magic is broken up into three primary categories. Arcane, Naturae, and Martial.

Arcane mana is an invisible substance that permeates the air and flows through every realm in existence. Souls naturally draw this substance in like a magnet, only for it to flow back out again in a never ending cycle. Not many can harness it, but all have at least some Arcane mana traveling through their soul at all times. Mages are capable of manipulating their souls to take hold of Arcane mana and shape it while it's leaving their body. The way they shape it defines how the mana is processed and what kind of spell they end up using, and the size and power of their soul dictates how powerful the spell can be. Attempting to cast a spell more powerful than your soul can handle can tear it apart. Similarly if you use too much magic in too short a time span you will wear down your soul and eventually either become unable to cast magic, or enter a comatose state. In the worst case scenario overworking your soul could also lead to death.

The next type of magic is known as Naturae, or life magic for the plebians out there. It's a unique type of mana that, rather than simply existing, is produced by all living things. It is also exceedingly dangerous. In using Naturae one could take control of a life, bring the wrath of nature itself down on some poor fucker, or even summon the dead to head your commands in some cases. Due to the dangerous and unpredictable nature of Naturae magic most have become quite wary of those who can wield it.

Finally we have Martial magic. By altering the shape of their soul they can change the course of the mana flowing through their bodies, giving them some additional advantages over the classes that can actually utilize actual magic.
WARNING: attempting to change the fundamental shape of your soul is a VERY bad idea unless you know EXACTLY what you're doing. When a mage uses magic they use their soul to process the mana inside of them and shape it on it's way out to form their target spell, they don't actually do anything that changes or moves the soul unnaturally. The reason it's so dangerous to change the shape of the soul is because when you do you could cause mana blockages in the body, resulting in affects similar to if you poured lead into a person's bloodstream. Mana becomes very volatile when forced to hold still in one spot. On top of essentially poisoning the person, as it condenses due to the blockage and gains a high enough concentration it can also begin processing on it's own, resulting in additional random effect(s) or just mutating the body of the individual, kind of like wild magic. That is, assuming they live long enough to get to that stage and don't seek help from a highly skilled healer to fix their soul. So anything from transforming into a monster to exploding into a massive fireball can happen. Moral of the story? Don't fuck with souls. You won't like what happens.

Combat

Combat in Loreheim works a lot like combat in D&D.

Combat will be turn based, each round taking 12 seconds, starting with initiative which is simply a d20 + Dexterity mod. During a round each player will have 2 actions/at will (think of at wills as bonus actions) abilities and 1 reaction that they can make use of. Base movement is also 20ft per turn and counted separately from your actions unless you want to move further than your base speed will allow, in which case you move an additional 20ft per action spent.



Familiars

Anyone who has the capability to wield magic can form the familiar bond with any willing creature of similar strength. The process can take upwards of multiple days of meditation to complete, but once created the familiar bond is something that can't be easily broken as it links your soul with the soul of the creature. The familiar will gain new abilities and strength as it draws its power from you to grow. Familiars level with you gain special abilities related to your own class, or classes, but if they die you will suffer greatly from the shattering of the bond.

Familiars have the ability to communicate mentally with it's soul bonded, on top of that you can look through your familiars eyes, hear through their ears, and experience what their other senses experience. This action does leave you vulnerable though since your senses will be replaced with the familiars.

The Point System and How It Works

Calculator for points:
Number of Encounters/day (Number of Turns in an average fight * number of abilities expected to be used for turn) All multiplied by 2/3

Magic and limited resource abilities like magic will be based off a point system. Players will get a certain number of points per day to spend. This applies mostly to spells and spell like abilities though. On top of magic not having a set number of spell slots there also isn't a set amount of damage dice, buffs, etc. There will instead be a base line and a number of points it costs to use that spell. Players can use more points to increase the spells potency, but should be wary of going overboard since points can't be regained until after a long rest.

Catalyst Rules for bonded creatures

You can try to bond with beasts of 8 or lower intelligence that you encounter by making an animal handling check against the CR of a creature + its Attitude modifier (indicated below). On a successful check, you have bonded with the beast. Bonded creatures are loyal to you, but still have a will of their own and can choose to attempt to shatter the bond. Additional changes to the DC can and will be made at the discretion of the DM depending on the situation.

Aggressive = 22 -or- Defensive = 18

Indifferent = 14

Friendly = 11

You can only perform a check on a creature to improve its attitude once per hour. If you fail you have to wait an hour to try again. In this way, to calm a creature from Aggressive or Defensive to Friendly, it would take 3 successful checks over 2 hours.
After the beast has become friendly you must spend 1d6 + creature CR hours meditating to achieve a full bond. If you're interrupted during this process you'll have to start over.

Intelligent bonds: If you wish to bond with a creature with 10 or higher intellect similar rules will apply, but rather than animal handling checks, you'll be making persuasion checks. The creature must also be completely willing, and for some they won't be willing to bond with a person no matter how high the player roles. Since they have intelligence they will understand that they'll be in a subservient position, and not all entities are willing to serve anyone but themselves.

Balancing Enemy HP:

First, calculate the average damage output for the party. Each party member has two actions, so if there are three party members then about 4 to 6 attacks will be made each turn on average most likely. Taking into account the dice being fickle about 3 attacks should hit each round. Lets say each attack has the ability to do 6 damage, that'll be about 18. So a good average would be 9 damage per turn.

Now to determine how long the enemy should last. On average I like combat to last between 6 and 8 rounds. So, if the party is doing about 9 damage per round the enemy should have between 54 and 72 health.



Balancing Enemy Damage Output

Applying the same per turn damage logic for determining an enemies health, you need to take total health of the front line party member(s) and compare it to how long they need to last in order to not die. If there is one front liner and they have 20 max HP and the fight should last for 6 to 8 rounds then the enemy should be doing 3 to 4 damage each turn, so each attack should do 1 to 2 damage.

Now, obviously there could be a lot more factors than that. Lets say there are two front liners and they each have 20 max HP. With the previously mentioned desired 6 to 8 round combats and the enemy splitting it's attention between the two the damage should be increased to reflect the higher survivability the party members might have by being able to share the damage without making it too easy. Using the same average as before we could probably increase the damage for each attack to about 2 to 3.


Levels

So the way levels are going to work will be similar to normal D&D, but also kind of not. When you gain enough XP, you level up and get a selection of active and passive abilities to choose from.

For example...
Level 1: 3 actives, 2 passives, and any class features
Level 2: 1 upgrade point, and 1 stat point
Level 3: 2 actives, 1 passive

Actives are like spells and abilities, like fireball for example, and passives will be passive effects that help the characters in much more minor ways, like a small boost to certain types of damage, or darkvision out of 10ft. Stuff like that.
Finally, upgrade points can be used to level up an active or passive you've already taken, or you can use it to gain a new active or passive from lower levels.

Every level is 150 experience apart, the reason for this is that the more experienced you gain, the less you have to learn. Thus, as players progress and fight their way through life they will have to adapt and get more creative in order to gain more experience. They'll have to try new things and put in actual effort to get further along.

The way I'll be handing out XP will be based upon the players actions. Everything action and encounter has the potential to grant XP, but only if the players work for it. Due to that XP will be rewarded based upon a players actions rather than a set amount.

Conditions

Blinded

A blinded creature can't see and automatically fails any ability check that requires sight. Attack rolls against the creature have advantage, and the creature's Attack rolls have disadvantage.


Charmed

A charmed creature can't attack the charmer or target the charmer with harmful Abilities or Magical Effects. The charmer has advantage on any ability check to interact socially with the creature.


Deafened

A deafened creature can't hear and automatically fails any ability check that requires hearing.


Frightened

A frightened creature has disadvantage on ability checks and attack rolls while the source of its fear is within Line of Sight. The creature can't willingly move closer to the source of its fear.


Grappled

A grappled creature's speed becomes 0, and it can't benefit from any bonus to its speed. The condition ends if the Grappler is incapacitated. The condition also ends if an Effect removes the grappled creature from the reach of the Grappler or Grappling Effect, such as when a creature is hurled away by a spell.


Incapacitated

An incapacitated creature can't take actions or reactions.


Invisible

An invisible creature is impossible to see without the aid of magic or a Special sense. For the Purpose of hiding, the creature is heavily obscured. The creature's Location can be detected by any noise it makes or any tracks it leaves. Attack rolls against the creature have disadvantage, and the creature's Attack rolls have advantage.


Paralyzed

A paralyzed creature is incapacitated and can't move or speak. The creature automatically fails Strength and Dexterity saving throws. Attack rolls against the creature have advantage. Any attack that hits the creature is a critical hit if the attacker is within 5 feet of the creature.


Petrified

A petrified creature is Transformed, along with any objects it is wearing or carrying, into a solid inanimate substance (usually stone). Its weight increases by a factor of ten, and it ceases aging. The creature is incapacitated, can't move or speak, and is unaware of its surroundings. Attack rolls against the creature have advantage. The creature automatically fails Strength and Dexterity saving throws. The creature has resistance to all damage. The creature is immune to poison and disease, although a poison or disease already in its system is suspended, not neutralized.


Poisoned

A poisoned creature loses max hp equal to half the damage being dealt by the poison each round after the third round of being poisoned.


Prone

A prone creature's only movement option is to crawl, unless it stands up and thereby ends the condition. The creature has disadvantage on attack rolls. An attack roll against the creature has advantage if the attacker is within 5 feet of the creature. Otherwise, the Attack roll has disadvantage.


Restrained

A restrained creature's speed becomes 0, and it can't benefit from any bonus to its speed. Attack rolls against the creature have advantage, and the creature's Attack rolls have disadvantage. The creature has disadvantage on Dexterity saving throws.


Stunned

A stunned creature is incapacitated, can't move, and can speak only falteringly. The creature automatically fails Strength and Dexterity saving throws. Attack rolls against the creature have advantage.


Unconscious

An unconscious creature is incapacitated, can't move or speak, and is unaware of its surroundings The creature drops whatever it's holding and falls prone. The creature automatically fails Strength and Dexterity saving throws. Attack rolls against the creature have advantage. Any attack that hits the creature is a critical hit if the attacker is within 5 feet of the creature.
